Design space · stacking

Combining ideas

One QR can stay a simple scan while the card behind it pulls from several signed, revocable streams — each with its own authority.

The strongest combos keep the boundary clear: the game can reward discovery, but only the maintainer signs safety; the crisis card can show food, but the kitchen signs freshness.

How stacking works

The QR encodes a URL. The resolver returns one document composed of many signed streams. Each stream has a known signer and can be revoked on its own.

A scan does not mean one party speaks for everything. The maintainer signs maintenance. The kitchen signs freshness. The game signs quest meta. The coalition signs the resource list. If a stream goes stale or wrong, only that stream is revoked — the object stays scannable.

  • Authority is visible Each stream shows who signed it and what their key can say — not a generic “verified” stamp
  • Revoke one stream, keep the rest A recalled batch disappears from the food list without killing the wallet card
  • Safety copy outranks game copy When a maintainer stream says out-of-service, no game overlay can paint it green

What makes a combo strong

Weak stacks

  • One signer claims authority for everything on the page
  • Game copy and safety copy share a styling and tone
  • Vendor reprints a sticker every time anything changes
  • Revoke kills the object instead of one stream
  • Scan log retained “for analytics” by default

Strong stacks

  • Each stream names its signer and what it can attest
  • Safety stream visually outranks discovery stream
  • Vendor updates one state object — no reprinting
  • Revoke is per-stream; sibling streams keep working
  • No scan-trail retention; honest about limits

A QR is a pointer. A stack of signed streams is what makes one printed mark stay honestly useful as the world around it changes.
