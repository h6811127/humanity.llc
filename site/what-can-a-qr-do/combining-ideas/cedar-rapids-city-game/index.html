Over one weekend, Cedar Rapids starts behaving like programmable public space. Relay arches, riverwalk drops, bridge warnings, witness seals, and hidden cabinets can all coexist: some fight over territory, some coordinate trust, and some wake only when the city solves them together.

Rule design · cooperation beats exploitation

Use game theory so the strongest move is helping the city wake up together, not hiding information or farming a private score.

  • Public goods mechanics Some objects only wake after a shared threshold — for example 20 site-code contributions unlocking the next clue for everyone.
  • Anti-hoarding rewards The first finder gets a seed clue, but the object evolves only after several more people scan it, so sharing beats secrecy.
  • Trust and vouch mechanics Deeper paths can require a vouch from a NewBo object, a local business, or a community steward without demanding legal identity.
  • Prisoner's-dilemma choices A player can reveal a clue privately now for a small win, or leave it hidden long enough for the group to unlock a better shared ending.
  • Scarcity without surveillance Use expiring clues, rotating QR states, and limits like 25 passes before sunset instead of tracking who went where.
  • Sybil resistance Rate limits, proof-of-control challenges, local physical codes, one-time signed tokens, business-issued vouches, and device-local limits curb scan spam without identity dossiers.
  • Coordination games Fragments from Czech Village, NewBo, and Greene Square can combine into the next state, rewarding city-scale coordination over solo optimization.

Data policy fit

Anonymous participation in shared urban memory.

This demo assumes no geo-tracking, no movement analytics, no social heatmaps, and no hidden engagement scoring. The resolver only needs the current signed object state: whether a relay is compromised, whether a trust chain is valid, whether a threshold has been met, whether a steward revoked the clue, and which public bulletins are live.

The city game should feel like anonymous participation in shared urban memory, not optimized behavioral telemetry. Sybil resistance should come from local friction like physical codes, one-time tokens, device-local ceilings, and business-issued vouches, not from building player dossiers. Public stewards can still publish closures, flood warnings, or cleanup holds when real space takes priority.

The best city game is a full coordination sandbox: relay contests, trust chains, compromise play, scarcity, and cooperative unlocks all change what physical objects reveal without creating a player dossier.
