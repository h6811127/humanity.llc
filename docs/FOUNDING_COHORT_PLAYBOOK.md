# Founding Cohort Playbook (Optional Early Testers)

**Status:** Supplemental — **non-gating**  
**Purpose:** Define how a small volunteer group can stress-test copy, scans, vouches, and support **before or during public launch**.

**Superseded for architecture and launch gates by:** `docs/PROTOCOL_FEDERATION_AND_LAUNCH_STRATEGY.md`

**Important:** Card creation is **public** when Phase A ships. This playbook does **not** control who may use the network. Tier 1 personalized artifacts are for **card holders**, not “accepted cohort members only.” See `docs/MERCH_LED_V1.md`.

---

## Goal

The optional early tester pool exists to answer one question:

> Does the Humanity Card trust loop make sense to real people in real contexts?

It is not a vanity waitlist, fake governance body, paid status tier, or **invite gate** to the product.

---

## Recommended Size

Start with 25 people.

Expand toward 100 only after:

- Card creation works.
- QR scanning works on mobile Safari.
- Vouching is understandable.
- Revocation is understandable.
- Live control proof is either implemented or clearly deferred.
- The support burden is manageable.

---

## Who To Invite

Prioritize people who can test a real use case:

- People who run or attend meetups.
- Cooperative organizers.
- Mutual-aid organizers.
- Open-source/privacy people.
- Artists/designers who understand social objects.
- Community moderators.
- Club or local group operators.
- People willing to give blunt feedback.

Avoid over-indexing on people who merely agree with the politics but will not use the product.

---

## Cohort Responsibilities

Founding cohort members should:

- Create a Humanity Card.
- Scan other cards on mobile Safari.
- Test whether the page is understandable in under 30 seconds.
- Request and give vouches only when they understand the responsibility.
- Test live control proof if available.
- Revoke a test QR credential.
- Explain what the card does and does not prove.
- Report confusing copy or misleading status.
- Test one physical QR artifact if available.
- Help review the constitution and trust model after using the product.

They should not:

- Sell vouches.
- Claim founding status means legal identity verification.
- Claim buying an artifact verifies anyone.
- Treat a sticker as proof of identity.
- Present bootstrap governance as mature democracy.

---

## Week 0: Preparation

Before inviting the cohort:

- Publish the product trust model.
- Publish the founding bootstrap rule.
- Publish the forbidden claims list.
- Prepare a card creation flow.
- Prepare mobile Safari scan pages.
- Prepare a vouching flow or vouching simulation.
- Prepare a revocation demo.
- Prepare support/report channels.
- Prepare a feedback form.

---

## Week 1: Card Creation And Scan

Tasks:

1. Create a card.
2. Save/export key backup if available.
3. Scan the card on mobile Safari.
4. Read the public card page.
5. Explain what the card proves.
6. Explain what the card does not prove.

Success criteria:

- 80% of testers can create a card without help.
- 80% can scan a card and understand active/revoked/suspended/unknown status.
- 80% do not describe the product as legal ID or a generic QR profile.

---

## Week 2: Vouching

Tasks:

1. Request a vouch from someone who actually knows you.
2. Review what a vouch means.
3. Give a vouch only if the statement is true.
4. Inspect the public vouch summary.
5. Revoke a test vouch if supported.

Success criteria:

- Testers understand `Vouched Human`.
- Testers understand a vouch is social trust, not legal identity.
- Testers understand vouches are limited and accountable.
- Testers do not expect vouch counts to become a popularity score.

---

## Week 3: Live Control And Revocation

Tasks:

1. Scanner opens a card.
2. Scanner requests live control proof.
3. Owner signs the challenge.
4. Scanner sees recent proof.
5. Owner revokes a test QR credential.
6. Scanner sees the revoked state.

If live control proof is deferred, test the copy and prototype instead.

Success criteria:

- Testers understand live control means recent key control.
- Testers do not interpret live control as legal identity.
- Testers understand revocation changes future scan results but not physical reality.

---

## Week 4: Real-World Context

Each tester should answer:

- Where would you use this?
- Who would you scan?
- Who would scan you?
- Why would someone ask for live control?
- What community would benefit from this?
- Would you invite someone else? Who?

Success criteria:

- At least 5 testers name a real use case.
- At least 5 testers invite another person without being pressured.
- At least 1 event, club, cooperative, or online community wants a pilot conversation.

---

## Founding Rituals

Useful rituals:

- First card creation session.
- First scan circle.
- First vouch circle.
- First revocation demo.
- First live control demo.
- First physical card/sticker scan.
- First public feedback session.
- First constitution/trust model review after product use.

Rituals should make the trust model memorable. They should not imply ceremony verification unless a ceremony credential process exists.

---

## Public Sharing Guidance

Good public sharing:

- "I created a Humanity Card."
- "I am helping test a non-surveillance trust card."
- "This is founder-built today, member-governed by design."
- "My card can be revoked and exported."
- "This does not replace legal ID."

Avoid:

- "I am officially verified."
- "This proves who I am."
- "I bought a verified card."
- "This is decentralized already."
- "This is impossible to fake."

---

## Feedback Questions

Ask after every session:

1. What was confusing?
2. What felt trustworthy?
3. What felt like overclaiming?
4. What would make you use this again?
5. What would make you invite someone?
6. What would make you distrust it?
7. Where would you actually use this?

---

## Graduation Criteria

The early tester pool is working if:

- People create cards.
- People scan cards.
- People request/give vouches.
- People understand revocation.
- People understand live control proof.
- People invite others.
- At least one real community wants a pilot.
- The trust model becomes easier to explain, not harder.
