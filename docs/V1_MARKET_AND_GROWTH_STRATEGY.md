# V1 Market And Growth Strategy

**Status:** Strategic draft  
**Purpose:** Evaluate Humanity Commons through a venture/product lens and define the market wedge, failure modes, viral loops, and validation gates before overbuilding.

**Launch and architecture:** `docs/PROTOCOL_FEDERATION_AND_LAUNCH_STRATEGY.md` (public launch, federated networks - not cohort gate).

**Direction:** `docs/DEMOCRATIC_INFRASTRUCTURE.md`  -  sell the **trust loop and org layer**, not a static QR profile. Phase A MVP will look minimal; beachheads must still name a workflow that needs **live revoke + vouch + check-in**, not Linktree.

---

## Investor-Style Verdict

Humanity Commons is a strong product thesis, but it is not yet a venture-obvious company.

The idea is worth prototyping because the macro problem is real: AI-generated accounts, bots, platform-owned reputation, surveillance-heavy verification, and collapsing online trust. A signed, revocable, non-surveillance Humanity Card with vouching and live control proof is differentiated.

The project is not yet venture-ready because the first wedge, buyer, recurring use case, and growth loop still need proof. The v1 question is not "Can this be built?" It is:

> Do strangers and communities use the trust loop (create, scan, vouch, revoke) without hand-holding - and can a second operator adopt the same spec?

Until that is proven, Humanity Commons should be treated as a high-conviction prototype and public-interest protocol experiment, not a scaled venture business.

**AI product stance:** Macro problem includes AI/bot spam; product response is **signed, revocable trust loop** — not an AI profile platform. Optional L3 explain consumes signed snapshot only ([`AI_FEATURE_DEVELOPMENT.md`](AI_FEATURE_DEVELOPMENT.md)).

## Sharpest Market Wedge

Do not launch as "identity SaaS for everyone."

The strongest early wedge is:

> Trust and membership infrastructure for communities that reject surveillance platforms.

Likely early adopters:

- Cooperatives.
- Mutual-aid groups.
- Unions and worker organizations.
- Local clubs and community groups.
- Privacy/open-source communities.
- Events and meetups.
- Online communities facing AI/bot spam.
- Creators or organizers who want human membership without platform lock-in.

These groups are more likely to care about:

- No phone number requirement.
- No government ID requirement.
- No scan analytics.
- Exportability.
- Member-governed infrastructure.
- Transparent trust rules.

They are also more likely to forgive an early product if the trust model is honest and useful.

## First Beachhead Options

### Option A: Events And Meetups

**Use case:** Attendees carry a Humanity Card, scan each other, request live control proof, and build vouches after meeting.

**Why it could work:**

- In-person scanning makes live control proof legible.
- Stickers/cards have a natural role.
- Public card creation can be tested quickly in person.

**Risk:**

- Event organizers may not need this unless it solves check-in, member trust, or follow-up.

### Option B: Cooperative And Member Organizations

**Use case:** Organizations use Humanity Cards for member onboarding, consent-based directories, event participation, and future voting/assembly workflows.

**Why it could work:**

- Strong mission fit.
- Clear future buyer.
- Governance values matter.

**Risk:**

- Sales cycles and feature requirements may be heavier than v1 can support.

### Option C: Online Communities Facing AI/Bot Pressure

**Use case:** Communities use Humanity Cards as an optional non-surveillance proof that a participant is vouched and can prove live control.

**Why it could work:**

- Strong "why now" because AI spam is getting worse.
- Digital distribution can be faster than local organizing.

**Risk:**

- Vouching may not feel strong enough for high-abuse communities.

### Recommended V1 Beachhead

Start with **events/meetups and public launch**, then approach cooperative/member organizations - including as a **second network operator**.

Reason: events make the trust loop physically understandable. A person can scan a card, see `Vouched Human`, ask for live control, and understand why a static sticker is not enough. A co-op running the same API proves federation, not startup capture.

---

## Core Job-To-Be-Done

The v1 job should be concrete:

> Let a real person show a signed public card, get vouched for by known humans, and prove live control without giving a platform their phone, ID, social graph, or scan analytics.

Avoid positioning v1 as:

- A universal identity layer.
- A legal ID replacement.
- A bot-proof system.
- A full community operating system.
- A merch brand.

## How The Project Could Fail

### No Urgent Use Case

People may understand the idea but not know where they would actually use it.

**Mitigation:** Pick one launch context, such as founding events or member org onboarding, and design the demo around that context.

### Looks Like A QR Profile

If users summarize the product as "Linktree with politics," the trust thesis is not visible enough.

**Mitigation:** Center signed card status, vouches, live control proof, revocation, and artifact warnings. De-emphasize generic profile customization.

### Vouching Feels Weak

Users may not believe that social vouches prove anything meaningful.

**Mitigation:** Use `Vouched Human`, show vouch recency, enforce quotas/waiting periods, and test with public launch plus a small accountable early tester pool if needed.

### Onboarding Feels Too Heavy

The product may ask users to understand keys, governance, vouching, and revocation before they feel value.

**Mitigation:** Keep the first user path simple: create card, scan card, get vouched, prove control. Move governance detail into linked pages.

### Cold Start

Vouching depends on trusted humans existing before the network is trusted.

**Mitigation:** Use visible bootstrap rules for 10-25 founding humans or stewards. Do not pretend the first trust network is already decentralized.

### Artifacts Become Merch

Physical products may generate attention but not actual trust usage.

**Mitigation:** Launch one excellent QR artifact tied to the trust loop. Delay broad store expansion until people scan and use the cards.

### Governance Overwhelms Product

The cooperative roadmap can make the project feel abstract or politically heavy before the product works.

**Mitigation:** Lead with the practical trust tool. Let ownership, transparency, and governance be credible support, not the first screen.

### No Clear Buyer

Individuals may like the card but not pay enough to sustain development; organizations may need features beyond v1.

**Mitigation:** Validate both user desire and organizational buyer interest separately. Do not confuse artifact purchases with recurring business demand.

### Trust Incident

False vouches, impersonation, harassment, or stolen QR confusion could undermine credibility early.

**Mitigation:** Launch narrow, with public abuse rules, revocation UI, report paths, and careful copy.

### Venture Misalignment

Traditional VC incentives may pressure the project toward surveillance, aggressive growth, or control rights that conflict with the commons.

**Mitigation:** Prefer customer revenue, grants, donations, member loans, recoverable grants, capped revenue-based financing, or non-voting instruments unless a capital partner accepts the governance constraints.

## How It Could Become Viral

Humanity Commons becomes viral if the card becomes a simple status object with moral clarity:

> I can prove I am a real participant without becoming a data product.

### Viral Loops

**Founding card loop:** people create attractive cards and share screenshots.

**Vouch loop:** users invite trusted friends because vouching requires other humans.

**Live proof loop:** in-person scanning becomes memorable when someone asks, "Can you prove control right now?"

**Physical artifact loop:** stickers/cards spark scans in the real world.

**AI backlash loop:** users share the message "Prove you're real without handing over your phone, ID, or social graph."

**Community adoption loop:** one event, co-op, or group uses Humanity Cards, and members carry the card into adjacent communities.

**Anti-platform narrative loop:** the public line is easy to repeat: "No phone. No ID. No ads. No tracking."

## Public Lines To Test

Short lines:

- A public card for real humans.
- Prove you're real without becoming a data product.
- No phone. No ID. No ads. No tracking.
- A signed card you control.
- Human trust without platform surveillance.

Longer line:

> Humanity Commons lets you create a signed public card, get vouched for by real people, prove live control when needed, and carry a QR that always resolves to current status.

Do not use lines that imply:

- Legal identity.
- Guaranteed uniqueness.
- Bot-proof verification.
- Purchased status.
- Government-grade identity.

## Validation Gates

### 30-Day Prototype Gate

Continue only if:

- 25 people create cards.
- 10 people participate in vouching.
- 10 people correctly understand that a sticker does not prove identity.
- 5 people use or clearly want live control proof.
- At least 5 people can name a real context where they would use the card.
- Users do not describe it as "just a QR profile."

### 60-Day Market Gate

Continue toward public launch only if:

- At least one event, cooperative, club, or community wants to pilot it.
- Users invite others for vouches without being asked.
- Some users are willing to pay for a founding card/sticker while understanding it does not buy verification.
- At least one potential organization buyer describes a concrete workflow Humanity Commons could support.
- Support and trust incidents remain manageable.

### Venture Readiness Gate

Do not pitch as a venture-scale company until there is evidence of:

- A repeatable beachhead use case.
- A clear buyer or funder.
- Retention beyond novelty.
- A growth loop that does not depend on paid ads or ideology alone.
- A revenue path that does not violate the trust model.
- A governance/capital structure that can accept outside funding without sacrificing user rights.

---

## Strategic Recommendation

Build the narrow alpha. Do not overbuild the store, governance machinery, or broad identity platform.

The first public proof should be:

> Public launch users can create cards, vouch, scan, prove live control, revoke QR credentials, and explain why the system is not a QR profile, not merch-based verification, and not surveillance identity.
