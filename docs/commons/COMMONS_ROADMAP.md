# Democratic Socialist Enterprise Roadmap

**Status:** Strategic draft  
**Purpose:** Define how Humanity Commons can grow from a v1 trust product into a democratic, worker-and-member-governed digital enterprise.

**Trust layer architecture:** `docs/PROTOCOL_FEDERATION_AND_LAUNCH_STRATEGY.md`  -  federated networks and open standards are the path to institutional gravity; a single permanent operator or public blockchain core is not the plan.

---

## Executive Vision

Humanity Commons should become public-interest digital infrastructure owned and governed by the people who build it, use it, and depend on it.

The v1 product direction is Commons Pass: a mobile web membership pass for democratic communities, built on the Humanity Card credential primitive. The long-term enterprise is broader:

- A member-governed identity and trust commons.
- A community membership and credential infrastructure layer.
- A cooperative digital commerce and fulfillment layer.
- A network of local stewards, ceremonies, and mutual-aid workflows.
- A worker-owned operating company or cooperative that builds and maintains the software.
- A user/member assembly that governs rights-affecting standards.
- A non-extractive revenue model that funds operations without selling user data or concentrating control.

The phrase "digital democratic socialist enterprise" should mean concrete institutional design:

- Democratic ownership.
- Transparent finances.
- One-person-one-voice governance where user rights are affected.
- Worker power over labor conditions.
- Member power over standards and platform direction.
- Surplus reinvested into the commons before private extraction.
- Strong limits on investor control, wage inequality, surveillance, and enclosure.

This is not a demand to make v1 politically heavy. V1 should remain practical, trustworthy, and legible. The enterprise model should grow behind it as the community, revenue, and governance capacity become real.

---

## North Star

Build the trust, coordination, and commerce infrastructure that democratic communities need, without reproducing the platform economy's default pattern:

```text
users create value -> platform captures data and margin -> investors control the institution
```

Humanity Commons should instead move toward:

```text
members create value -> commons captures durable infrastructure -> workers and members govern surplus
```

## Founding Commitments

These commitments should guide every stage, including before the formal cooperative structure exists.

### 1. No Surveillance Business Model

Revenue must not depend on scan analytics, behavior tracking, identity brokerage, ad targeting, or selling user graphs.

### 2. No Pay-To-Be-Human

Purchases, subscriptions, donations, or merchandise must never grant human verification.

### 3. No Investor Control Of User Rights

Funding can support development, but user rights, protocol standards, revocation rules, suspension rules, and data governance must not be controlled by capital providers.

### 4. Democratic Exit

Users must be able to export identity data, QR history, public credentials, and verification records in documented formats.

### 5. Worker And Member Power

The people doing the labor and the people governed by the system need formal power. Advisory vibes are not enough.

### 6. Transparent Books

At maturity, revenue, margins, compensation bands, reserves, grants, debt, and surplus allocation should be visible to members.

### 7. Local Autonomy With Shared Standards

Local groups should be able to operate ceremonies, events, and trust practices, but shared standards protect portability and user rights.

### 8. Product First, Movement Close Behind

The card must be useful before the movement can scale. The movement narrative should give people a reason to care, share, and govern, but the core scan page must remain practical and understandable.

## Enterprise Thesis

Humanity Commons can become a sustainable enterprise because it combines four durable needs:

1. **Portable community membership:** People need ways to carry belonging, roles, credentials, and participation across communities without platform lock-in.
2. **Portable human trust:** People need ways to signal personhood, membership, reputation, and affiliation outside centralized social networks.
3. **Low-surveillance coordination:** Communities need tools that do not turn every interaction into analytics and ad inventory.
4. **Physical-digital belonging:** Passes, QR codes, cards, stickers, event kits, and printed artifacts make the system socially visible and fundable.
5. **Democratic infrastructure:** Cooperatives, unions, mutual-aid groups, communities, and local institutions need software they can trust politically, not just technically.

The market wedge is not "identity SaaS for everyone." The wedge is a trusted, member-owned membership and coordination layer for communities that already distrust extractive platforms.

The first market proof should be narrow: one event, cooperative, club, or online community should use the card because it solves a concrete trust or membership problem - with **public card creation** and a path to a **second network operator**. The project should not present itself as venture-ready until a repeatable beachhead use case, buyer/funder, retention signal, and non-extractive growth loop exist.

## Market Wedge And Beachhead

Humanity Commons should start where its values are a product advantage, not a distraction.

Strong early adopter segments:

- Events and meetups where people can scan, vouch, and prove live control in person.
- Cooperatives and member organizations that need trust infrastructure without platform lock-in.
- Mutual-aid and local community groups that need portable membership and consent boundaries.
- Privacy/open-source communities that already distrust phone-number and ID-based verification.
- Online communities under AI/bot pressure that need a humane alternative to surveillance verification.

Recommended v1 beachhead:

> Commons Pass for events and founding communities first; cooperative/member organization pilots second.

Events make the trust loop legible. A person can show a pass, scan a QR, ask for live control, check in, and receive a signed event stamp in one interaction. That experience is easier to validate than an abstract identity platform.

## Product Evolution

### Stage 1: Commons Pass And Humanity Card Trust Layer

**Goal:** Prove that a community can issue mobile web passes, scan members, check people into events, issue stamps, and rely on Humanity Cards for live control without phone, email, government ID, follower counts, ads, or scan analytics.

**Products:**

- Commons Pass.
- Community profile.
- Member pass issuance.
- Humanity Card.
- Public QR network.
- Vouching.
- Live control proof for contexts where static QR is not enough.
- Event check-in.
- Signed community stamps.
- Revocation and suspension status pages.
- Export bundle.

**Enterprise value:**

- Establishes the trust primitive.
- Establishes a concrete community workflow.
- Establishes the non-surveillance brand promise.
- Creates first member cohort.
- Creates a path to community-funded revenue.

### Stage 2: Cooperative Storefront And Artifact Economy

**Goal:** Turn physical artifacts into a sustainable, transparent revenue loop without making commerce the source of verification.

**Products:**

- Story-row storefront.
- Personalized community passes, QR stickers/cards, and event artifacts.
- Limited founding drops.
- Event kits.
- Governance-approved templates.
- Order support and reprint policies.

**Enterprise value:**

- Funds operations through visible, voluntary purchases.
- Builds social presence.
- Creates first operational playbook for production, support, and member trust.

### Stage 3: Member Trust Network

**Goal:** Make verification socially meaningful through vouching, ceremonies, local stewards, and transparent governance.

**Products:**

- Vouch request and vouch management.
- Ceremony credential workflows.
- Steward dashboards.
- Transparency reports.
- Local cohort tools.
- Appeal and suspension process.

**Enterprise value:**

- Builds network effects without surveillance.
- Makes membership meaningful.
- Creates a governance moat: the institution is trusted because members can inspect and influence it.

### Stage 4: Cooperative Operating System

**Goal:** Expand from individual cards into tools for democratic organizations.

**Products:**

- Member directories with consent layers.
- Assembly voting and deliberation integrations.
- Event check-in without identity brokerage.
- Mutual-aid matching with consent boundaries.
- Local chapter tooling.
- Federated network/operator support.

**Enterprise value:**

- Opens institutional revenue.
- Makes the platform useful to cooperatives, unions, clubs, clinics, community groups, and campaigns.
- Moves from "profile product" to "democratic coordination infrastructure."

### Stage 5: Federated Commons Network

**Goal:** Transition from one organization running the system to a federation of operators governed by shared standards.

**Products:**

- Multi-operator network support.
- Operator certification.
- Standards ratification process.
- Interoperable exports/imports.
- Local governance modules.
- Shared treasury and grants.

**Enterprise value:**

- Reduces single-point control.
- Supports global/local growth.
- Makes the commons resilient beyond one company.

## Business Model

Revenue must be aligned with trust. The best revenue is paid voluntarily by people and organizations who want the commons to exist.

Near-term revenue is not the same thing as venture proof. Artifact purchases, founding memberships, grants, and donations can fund the prototype, but venture-scale readiness requires evidence of repeatable demand, retention, and a buyer or funder who needs the product beyond its novelty.

### Near-Term Revenue

**1. Physical artifacts**

Revenue from stickers, cards, event kits, and limited drops. Margins should be transparent and include production, shipping/tax where applicable, platform/payment fees, support/reprint reserve, and commons operating margin.

**2. Founding memberships**

Optional memberships for early supporters. Membership should fund the commons, not buy verification or status. Benefits can include governance participation, early feature access, member calls, and public supporter badges if governed.

**3. Grants and aligned donations**

Apply for grants related to open source, privacy, cooperatives, democracy tech, public-interest technology, and local community infrastructure.

### Mid-Term Revenue

**4. Cooperative SaaS**

Paid tools for democratic organizations:

- Member onboarding.
- Consent-based directories.
- Event check-in.
- Credential issuance.
- Assembly participation.
- Exportable member records.

Pricing should be scaled by organization size and ability to pay.

**5. Support contracts**

Organizations can pay for setup, training, hosting assistance, custom templates, and governance implementation support.

**6. Ceremony and event infrastructure**

Sell or support event kits, credential flows, check-in tools, and local steward operations.

### Long-Term Revenue

**7. Federation dues**

Independent operators and chapters contribute dues to shared standards, security work, audits, and protocol maintenance.

**8. Public-interest infrastructure contracts**

Municipal, nonprofit, union, cooperative, and community institutions can fund deployments with strict data and governance terms.

**9. Commons treasury**

A member-governed treasury receives surplus and allocates funds to maintenance, mutual aid, grants, local chapters, open-source development, and reserves.

## What Not To Monetize

These should remain off-limits:

- Scan analytics.
- Ad targeting.
- Data brokerage.
- Selling verification.
- Pay-to-rank profiles.
- Selling access to private or semi-public identity layers.
- Investor-controlled protocol changes.
- Exclusive identity partnerships that break portability.
- Mandatory subscriptions for basic identity export or revocation.

## Ownership And Governance Roadmap

### Phase A: Founder-Controlled Build With Public Commitments

**When:** Pre-v1 to early v1.

**Structure:**

- Existing company can hold operations temporarily.
- Constitution and standards are public.
- Bootstrap governance keys are named.
- Founder/operator powers are explicit and temporary.

**Risk:** Founder control becomes permanent by inertia.

**Mitigation:** Publish transition criteria before meaningful revenue or membership growth.

### Phase B: Steward Council And Member Ratification

**When:** Public trust loop and early revenue exist.

**Structure:**

- Form an initial steward council.
- Ratify constitution and technical standards.
- Define who can vote on what.
- Publish transparency reports.

**Decision domains:**

- User rights.
- Suspension and appeal process.
- Verification thresholds.
- Badge issuance rules.
- Revenue and surplus policy.

### Phase C: Multi-Stakeholder Cooperative Or Trust-Owned Company

**When:** The product has recurring revenue, active members, and real operational obligations.

**Possible legal structures to evaluate with counsel:**

- Multi-stakeholder cooperative.
- Worker cooperative with user/member governance body.
- LLC owned by a perpetual purpose trust.
- Cooperative holding company plus nonprofit standards foundation.
- Public benefit company with binding trust/coop conversion documents.

**Recommended direction:** A multi-stakeholder cooperative or trust-owned operating company with formal worker and user/member classes.

### Phase D: Federation

**When:** Independent communities want to operate parts of the network.

**Structure:**

- Local operators.
- Shared protocol standards.
- Certification and audits.
- Federation dues.
- Shared security and governance process.

## Membership Classes

A mature enterprise likely needs more than one member class.

### Worker Members

People who materially build and operate the system.

**Rights:**

- Workplace governance.
- Compensation policy.
- Hiring/firing process.
- Operational planning.
- Board seats or council seats.

### User Members

People with active Humanity Cards who opt into membership.

**Rights:**

- Vote on user rights.
- Ratify technical standards affecting identity, privacy, suspension, export, and consent.
- Elect user stewards.
- Participate in surplus-allocation decisions within defined scope.

### Steward Members

Trusted operators who handle verification, ceremonies, safety, appeals, or local chapters.

**Rights:**

- Credential issuance authority within rules.
- Appeal process participation.
- Local chapter governance.
- Standards feedback.

### Organization Members

Cooperatives, unions, nonprofits, clubs, community groups, or local institutions using the system.

**Rights:**

- Product roadmap input.
- Federation participation.
- Organization-specific admin tools.
- Limited voting power that cannot override individual user rights.

### Supporter Members

People who fund or support the commons without operational authority.

**Rights:**

- Transparency access.
- Member updates.
- Participatory budgeting if adopted.
- No special identity or verification privileges.

## Governance Design

Governance should be boring, explicit, and hard to capture.

### One-Person-One-Vote For Rights

Anything affecting identity rights should be governed by people, not capital:

- Data export.
- Revocation.
- Suspension.
- Scan logging.
- Verification rules.
- Public/private data boundaries.
- Protocol compatibility.

### Bicameral Checks For Mixed Domains

Some decisions affect both workers and users. Use a two-body model:

- Worker body approves labor and operational decisions.
- Member body approves user-rights and standards decisions.
- Joint approval required for major budget, ownership, or mission changes.

### Supermajority For Core Rights

Changes to sovereignty, consent, suspension, surveillance, and exit rights should require a higher threshold than ordinary product decisions.

### Transparent Delegation

Not every member can vote on every operational detail. Delegate to councils, but require:

- Public scope.
- Term limits.
- Recall process.
- Minutes or decision logs.
- Conflict disclosures.

## Capital Strategy

The financing strategy should preserve democratic control.

### Preferred Funding Sources

- Customer revenue.
- Grants.
- Donations.
- Recoverable grants.
- Community notes.
- Member loans.
- Non-voting redeemable preferred shares if legally appropriate.
- Revenue-based financing with caps.

### Venture Capital Readiness

Traditional venture capital may be structurally misaligned with a democratic, non-surveillance trust commons unless investors accept explicit limits on control, monetization, and user-rights governance.

Do not pursue venture funding as the default path until there is evidence of:

- A repeatable beachhead use case.
- A clear buyer, funder, or recurring revenue path.
- Retention beyond founding-cohort enthusiasm.
- A growth loop that does not depend on paid ads or ideology alone.
- A revenue model that does not monetize surveillance, status, or identity access.
- A governance structure that protects protocol rights from capital control.

### Avoid Or Strictly Limit

- Venture capital with control rights.
- Investor vetoes over governance.
- Liquidation preferences that force exit.
- Data monetization covenants.
- Exclusive platform partnerships.

### Capital Rule

Capital can be paid back. Capital cannot govern the commons.

---

## Surplus Allocation

At maturity, annual surplus should be allocated transparently.

Recommended buckets:

- Operating reserve.
- Worker compensation and benefits.
- Security and infrastructure.
- User/member support.
- Grants to local chapters.
- Mutual aid or solidarity fund.
- Open-source maintenance.
- Reinvestment in product.
- Patronage or member dividends if legally and financially appropriate.

Any patronage/dividend model must not reward surveillance, status buying, or exploitative growth.

---

## Compensation Principles

To remain democratic socialist in practice, the enterprise needs labor standards.

Recommended policies:

- Publish compensation bands internally, and eventually to members at aggregate level.
- Adopt a wage ratio cap once the team is large enough.
- Provide worker-member path after a defined contribution period.
- Separate founder recognition from permanent founder control.
- Budget for care, support, moderation, and operations, not just engineering.

## Major Risks

### Trust Confusion

People may think a sticker proves identity or that buying merchandise creates status.

**Response:** Strong scan UI copy, unique item QR revocation, no mutable verification labels printed on artifacts, and live proof for higher-trust contexts.

### Governance Theater

The project may claim democracy before members have real power.

**Response:** Publish exactly what is founder-controlled, what is member-controlled, and what transition criteria change that.

### Capital Capture

Funding may pressure the project toward surveillance, growth-at-all-costs, or investor control.

**Response:** Use grants, customer revenue, capped instruments, and member finance before traditional VC.

### Operational Drag

Fulfillment, support, appeals, and governance can consume more time than engineering.

**Response:** Launch narrow, document policies, build operator tooling, and keep manual production approval until automation is proven.

### Political Overload

The product could become harder to understand if every screen carries the full ideology.

**Response:** Let the product be simple. Put the politics into ownership, governance, transparency, and user rights.

### No Urgent Use Case

People may understand the idea but not know where they would actually use it.

**Response:** Pick one beachhead context and test whether the card solves a concrete trust or membership problem there.

### Weak Growth Loop

The project may attract admiration but not repeated use or invitations.

**Response:** Design around vouching, live control proof, public launch, and real-world scans. Measure stranger create rate, invitations, and repeated use - not just positive feedback.

## Next Step

Do not start by incorporating a cooperative. Start by proving the trust loop and publishing the transition promise.

Immediate work:

1. Ship v1 private alpha.
2. Publish a one-page ownership transition promise.
3. Define member classes in draft.
4. Track revenue and costs transparently from the first paid order.
5. Open public card creation; run optional early tester feedback; ratify constitution after real use.
6. Publish the product trust model so members can govern what the system claims.
7. Validate one beachhead use case before expanding the store or governance machinery.
8. Keep the scan page practical while letting the movement live in supporting pages, artifacts, events, and cohort rituals.

The enterprise becomes real when members have something working to govern.
